# Introduction

*MOSAIC: Multi-Omics Stochastic Alignment via Information-theoretic Coupling*

Single-cell multi-omics experiments measure complementary aspects of cellular identity — transcription, chromatin accessibility, surface protein expression — in ways that rarely co-occur in the same assay. Most atlases are single-modality: the Tabula Sapiens transcriptome atlas and the ENCODE chromatin-accessibility atlas describe overlapping cell populations through different molecular lenses, but no individual cell was measured in both. Integrating such unpaired datasets into a shared cell-level representation is now a standard preprocessing step for joint downstream analyses — cell-type discovery, regulatory inference, trajectory reconstruction — and a rapidly maturing methodological literature has emerged to address it [SCOT, uniPort, MultiVI, Harmony, LIGER, Seurat WNN].

A conspicuous absence from that literature is **calibrated per-cell uncertainty**. Every existing method returns a point-estimate alignment: for each cell in modality A, a single most-likely partner in modality B, possibly with a confidence rank but without a probabilistic interpretation. Downstream analyses therefore cannot distinguish a trustworthy alignment from an artifact. Incorrect matches propagate silently into joint clusters, regulatory predictions, and spatial deconvolutions; cell populations that exist in one modality but not the other get "matched" to whichever target cells happen to be nearest in the shared embedding, with no signal to flag the error.

We introduce **MOSAIC**, a method that turns unpaired multi-omics alignment into a well-defined probabilistic problem and reports a *cluster-resolved alignment entropy* as a calibrated per-cell uncertainty measure. The technical contributions are threefold:

1. **Information-bottleneck coupling.** Each modality is encoded by its own IB-VAE whose latent is constrained both to reconstruct the modality (via ZINB for RNA and binary cross-entropy for ATAC) and to predict a low-dimensional *cluster-centroid summary* of the partner modality. The cluster-centroid target is a critical design choice: naively using the partner cell's own feature vector causes catastrophic per-cell memorization, while the cluster-centroid formulation forces the latent to retain cluster identity — a generalizing signal — rather than individual cell fingerprints.

2. **Procrustes-based latent coupling.** The two independently-trained IB-VAE latents live in different rotations of the same underlying space; we fit an orthogonal Procrustes rotation on shared cluster centroids to bring them into a common frame. This post-hoc alignment is cheap, interpretable, and avoids the training complexity of methods that jointly optimize both modalities.

3. **Cluster-resolved alignment entropy.** We solve an entropic optimal-transport problem between the two latent clouds and, rather than reporting the per-row entropy of the transport plan (which we show is miscalibrated because of the geometry of dense clusters), we marginalize each row over the target modality's cluster labels. The resulting cluster-level distribution has a natural entropy that captures *cross-cluster* uncertainty — a property that is calibrated in the strict sense: it predicts (AUROC ≈ 0.89) which cells the method has assigned to the wrong cluster.

On three paired single-cell benchmarks — 10x Multiome PBMC 10k, E18 mouse Brain 5k, and CITE-seq PBMC (RNA + 14 surface proteins) — averaged over 3 random seeds at β=0.001, MOSAIC achieves: **FOSCTTM 0.049 ± 0.002 on Brain** (label-transfer 96.2%/96.6%, ARI 0.94 ± 0.003), **FOSCTTM 0.118 ± 0.008 on PBMC** (label transfer 91.3%/90.9%, ARI 0.724, 10-seed), and **FOSCTTM 0.091 ± 0.003 on CITE-seq** (label transfer 87.4%/95.1%, ARI 0.766 ± 0.018). MOSAIC outperforms SCOT by 22% on FOSCTTM and 105% on label transfer on PBMC, and by 89% on FOSCTTM and 7.2× on label transfer on Brain (where SCOT fails completely). The cluster-resolved alignment entropy correctly identifies the partner cluster for 96.4%–98.4% of cells and, in leave-one-cluster-out missing-type detection, achieves mean AUROC 0.960 (PBMC), 0.995 (Brain), and 0.946 (CITE-seq). Applied to five simulated clinical immune deficiency states on PBMC (CD8 T lymphopenia, NK cell deficiency, B cell aplasia, monocytopenia, Treg deficiency), MOSAIC detects each absent population with mean AUROC 0.952, demonstrating direct medical relevance without disease-specific training. This is the first method we are aware of that provides a per-cell uncertainty signal for unpaired single-cell multi-omics alignment.

The core insight is a negative result about naive entropic OT: cell-level row entropy on the transport plan is NOT a calibrated uncertainty measure. It anti-correlates with alignment error because it's confounded with target cluster density. Properly marginalizing away within-cluster variation — to obtain a *cluster-resolved* entropy — turns a noisy, miscalibrated statistic into a useful UQ signal. We believe this resolution generalizes beyond MOSAIC to any OT-based integration method that wants to report per-cell confidence.
